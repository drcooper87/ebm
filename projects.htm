Additive Manufacturing: Tracking the energy usage of new technologies

shengdec@mit.edu
corman@mit.edu
Additive manufacturing technologies use a digital design to build three-dimensional structures through sequential layering. Advantages include low material yield loss and the potential to manufacture lightweight products (often with complex geometries infeasible using traditional machining methods). Current drawbacks include limitations on process building rate and surface finish quality.

We are working with Cummins to analyse the resource and energy consumption of additive processes. We will compare it with conventional manufacturing and evaluate the feasibility of its application in injection molding tooling.

RAFFT Project: Modeling the benefits of a new incremental sheet forming technology

drcooper@mit.edu
keaton@mit.edu
Rapid and Flexible Forming Technology (RAFFT) is a new sheet metal forming process in which heavy fixed dies are replaced with computer controlled tools that press int the sheet metal to form a shape. The absence of eth heavy dies means that prototypes and custom componenst can be built cheaply, wuickly and without teh associated enviromental effecst of die production.

The project is part of a three-year, $7.04 million U.S. Department of Energy grant to advance next-generation, energy-efficient manufacturing processes. Led by Ford, other collaborators include Northwestern University, The Boeing Company, Massachusetts Institute of Technology and Penn State Erie. MIT are leading the collaboration in assessing the potential energy, carbon and cost benfits of the technology.

    See Ford's Rapid and Flexible Forming Technology



    We are working with Cummins to analyse the resource and energy consumption of additive processes. We will compare it with conventional manufacturing and evaluate the feasibility of its application in injection molding tooling.

    Additive manufacturing has developed rapidly in recent years and continues to gain importance. These technologies use a digital design to build three-dimensional structures through sequential layering. The flexibility and the ability to manufacture lightweight products with less raw materials are advantages of this new technology. In addition, additive manufacturing makes possible complex geometries that are very difficult or even infeasible using traditional machining methods. Currently there are certainly drawbacks, especially limitations on process building rate and surface finish quality, but additive technologies continue to improve, making possible an expanding range of applications.

    In order to evaluate the technologies in terms of sustainability, a deeper understanding and description of the systems are necessary. In this project, we work with Cummins to conduct a detailed analysis to characterize the resource and energy consumption of additive processes. By categorizing and studying different additive manufacturing methods, we will compare it with conventional manufacturing and evaluate the feasibility of its application in injection molding tooling. To fully assess the environmental impact of additive technologies, the analysis will include pre- and post-processing.

    My current research is on evaluating the potential energy, carbon and cost savings associated with the successful development of flexible sheet metal forming processes.
